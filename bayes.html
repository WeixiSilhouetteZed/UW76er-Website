Introduction to Bayesian Statistics

Over the course of undergraduate study at the University of Waterloo, I found that there is not a single statistics course on the Bayesian method (some of them provide partial introduction). So, we are basically a school of frequentists. This is not a set of diss tracks against the frequentist approach as that would be comparing apples to bananas. Providing a new perspective on an intuitive level is the main goal of this endeavour.

Disclaimer: The materials presented here are merely my summary work on a set of amazing YouTube videos created by ox educ. Their videos, created in 2014, are definitely of high quality and hidden gems just like a lot of other math/statistics series.

Moreover, some of the examples are found in some notable texts that you will find references as you read. I will try to keep it interesting as everything will be in the first-person narrative and I do not intend to write a dry textbook.

Prerequisites: any introductory course on probability theory and preferably also a course in basic statistics (frequentist version). At the University of Waterloo, those would be STAT231 and STAT230/330. Basically, make sure you know what PMF/PDF, CDF, and maximum likelihood methods mean. If you don't, there is no need to find another textbook or wait for another course, just go onto your local inter-web and Google those concepts.

Statistical Inference: Frequentist vs Bayesian

The idea of statistical inference is essentially a story of scarcity (I know I sound like an economist). Imagine you are the governor of a state and you want to know your citizens' opinions on gas pipeline construction. It could be a very simple problem like, "Agree or disagree?". Your state has a population of 1 million people. Say each survey costs $1 to administrate on average. If you want the "exact" opinion of the whole population, congratulations, you are going to spend 1 million tax dollars just to know what your people think at a "single" time point. Statisticians in the room (frequentist or Bayesian) might start laughing at your incompetence despite your initiative of goodwill. The moral of the story is, it is always costly to do anything with the whole population. What if you conduct your survey just on the 10% of the population of each city, a sample, and take whatever analysis/conclusion that you retrieve as an estimate of the "exact" result of the whole population? This is pretty much what statistical inference is usually used to do. Save tax dollars.

Remark My description of the statistical inference might seem superficial. But you get the idea. There is something in the quotation marks that might be interesting. The notion of an exact/real/true value of a population property might be even more superficial than one think. Such a quantity might never be observed even if the whole population is taken into account since the collection of data can never happen at an instance (in reality, might be years). This will be our focus next, a debate on how to treat such quantities.

Frequentist's Take

Back to the state government example from above, the frequentist statisticians would say, "you know what? That true value of the proportion of people who agree with your greedy pipeline construction, Î¸\thetaÎ¸ï»¿, is just a constant, but an unknown one. So don't ask me to figure out the exact value, I will try my best to estimate it. To do such estimations, so you do pay me, I am going to assume the potential samples as a bunch of random variables. I mean people's mind are so whimsical, might as well treat them as random. Let's just say Î¸=0.6\theta=0.6Î¸=0.6ï»¿. We better see around 60% of the sample survey shows positive results. Or even more precisely, out of 100 responses, we better see some frequency around 60 positive ones".

So what's the catch here? Frequentist, like literally, wants to perceive or estimate probability as proportion or frequencies. They believe that parameters, the holy Î¸\thetaÎ¸ï»¿, are unknown but constant on the infinite real line. Since the samples are considered random, somehow iid, for most of the cases, this allows them to consider estimators, pivotal quantities, confidence intervals, and corresponding hypothesis testing processes. All based on one simple idea, Î¸\thetaÎ¸ï»¿ is constant and frequencies from the sample help us estimate it.

What might be the problem? I have nothing against the idea of the parameter being an unknown constant since it is just a philosophical question on how do we think of the unknown. (Into the unknown! Into the unknown~~~!) The problem is about pragmatism, or more precisely, empirical viability. For example, one of the fundamental assumptions of the frequentist approach is each unit in the sample is sampled from the same distribution since Î¸\thetaÎ¸ï»¿ is constant. This could apply to a lot of situations but there is one that can make the point clear enough.

Consider your favourite coin-tossing game at your local pubs. It is an age-old question whether the loonie (this is a Canadian edition) is fair when it comes to coin tossing. When those statistics questions related to this topic described to you in an exam, you are always given the counts of head and tails, then you proceed to compute your maximum likelihood estimate or whatever other voodoo magic number. You probably don't have time to question this data's existence while apparently, they are having their own existential crisis. How in the fudging world that one can toss the coin 50 times with the exact same torque, air pressure, temperature, or any other quantities you learned from high school physics that might exert a force on the coin for each toss? This is empirically infeasible. After this example, if you apply similar thinking to other frequentist statistics questions, especially on how the sample is collected, you will find this approach collapse very abruptly.
